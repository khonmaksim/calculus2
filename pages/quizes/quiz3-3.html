QUIZ: Double Integrals in Polar Coordinates

Change the Cartesian integral into an equivalent polar integral and evaluate the polar integral.

Q1. \( \int_{-1}^{1} \int_{0}^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} dy dx \)

Solution for Q1

Step 1: Identify the given region. - The limits describe the upper semicircle of radius 1 centered at the origin. Step 2: Convert to polar coordinates. - Use transformations: \( x = r\cos\theta \), \( y = r\sin\theta \). - The limits transform to \( 0 \leq r \leq 1 \), \( 0 \leq \theta \leq \pi \). Step 3: Rewrite the integral in polar form. \[ \int_0^{\pi} \int_0^1 r dr d\theta \] Step 4: Evaluate the inner integral. \[ \int_0^1 r dr = \left[ \frac{r^2}{2} \right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2} \] Step 5: Evaluate the outer integral. \[ \int_0^{\pi} \frac{1}{2} d\theta = \frac{1}{2} \times \pi = \frac{\pi}{2} \]

Q2. \( \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{\sqrt{1-y^2}} (x^2 + y^2) dx dy \)

Solution for Q2

Step 1: Identify the given region. - The limits describe a quarter-circle of radius 1 in the first quadrant. Step 2: Convert to polar coordinates. - Use transformations: \( x = r\cos\theta \), \( y = r\sin\theta \). - The limits transform to \( 0 \leq r \leq 1 \), \( 0 \leq \theta \leq \frac{\pi}{2} \). Step 3: Rewrite the integral in polar form. \[ \int_0^{\pi/2} \int_0^1 r^3 dr d\theta \] Step 4: Evaluate the inner integral. \[ \int_0^1 r^3 dr = \left[ \frac{r^4}{4} \right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{4} \] Step 5: Evaluate the outer integral. \[ \int_0^{\pi/2} \frac{1}{4} d\theta = \frac{1}{4} \times \frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{\pi}{8} \]

Q3. \( \int_{0}^{2} \int_{0}^{\sqrt{4-y^2}} (x^2 + y^2) dx dy \)
